# Build a piece by hand

[Materializing a shape](../explanations/materializing-shapes.md) is the fast way to start, but you can also build a piece stitch by stitch. This is how you create something a generated shape can't give you, or learn exactly how a piece is constructed.

This guide assumes you have signed in and started a new pattern.

## 1. Choose how the piece starts

An empty pattern asks **How does this piece start?** Pick one:

- **Magic Ring** — a closed ring centre, the usual amigurumi start.
- **Open Round** — an open ring centre.
- **Foundation Chain** — a starting chain worked in two passes.

For a typical amigurumi, choose **Magic Ring**.

## 2. Build the opening round

After choosing Magic Ring, the controls bar switches to ring-building:

1. Pick a stitch type (usually `sc`) from the selector.
2. Click **+ stitch** to add each stitch to the ring. A magic ring needs a minimum number of stitches before it can be confirmed.
3. When the round looks right, click **Confirm**.

The opening round is set, and the editor switches to normal editing.

## 3. Add stitches and rounds

In normal editing, turn on the **append mode** checkbox in the controls bar so new stitches are added at the end of the piece. Then:

- **+ stitch** — add one stitch of the selected type.
- **End row** — finish the current round and begin the next.

As you add stitches, the round-count badge and the 3D preview update live.

## 4. Shape with increases and decreases

Shaping is what turns a tube into a ball or a cone. With **append mode** on:

- **inc** — work two stitches into the same base, adding one to the round (makes the piece wider).
- **dec** — work one stitch over two bases, removing one from the round (makes it narrower).

To build a whole round to a target size at once, use **Autobuild row**: enter the number of stitches you want, and AmiLab builds or rebuilds the round to match, distributing increases or decreases evenly.

:::tip[Let the count guide you]

The stitch-count badge on each round and the live preview tell you whether your shaping is doing what you intended. Increase steadily for a dome, hold the count even for a straight wall, and decrease to close a piece in.

:::
